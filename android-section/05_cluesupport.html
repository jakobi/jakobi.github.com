Coping with Android - Setup Alternatives (including OPTWARE, X11 Hacks)

Android Article 05 - ClueSupport Manifesto

State described: early 2012 unless specified differently Last change: 20120309
License: CC-BY-SA.

Milestones in Increasing and Extending Update Availability Despite Vendor Whims

(doubles nicely as the list of milestones for improving the speed of security fixes and updates)

Google and many embedded vendors are in the animal trade and have sold the GNU of the traditional GNU/Linux environment for space to a mere naked kernel Linux.

Unix Shell and Remote GUI Updates and ...

In the long run, to be used with new software, security patches and bug fixes, any device needs to permit at minimum a chroot with real package management (which Market is NOT BY FAR, not even for non-system components) and hopefully vnc. At the latest, chroot or similar must be available, when the vendor has obsoleted the device. Otherwise, the user degenerates to a mere remote-controlled usee and repeatedly replaces devices at the vendor's whim.

Some Android vendors do not offer any support/updates at all, so the buy-replacement-device-interval of their dreams might as well be called a monetary ZERO-DAY exploit.

OPTWARE, or better yet, a debian chroot (as installed on my Note) guarantees a vendor-independent level of real longterm support until either the device develops actual hardware issues or the user changes his device use patterns sufficiently to consider a new device (say, to read A4 documents full-screen without zooming).

Side benefits of a debian chroot on a modern Android like the Samsung Galaxy Note: a portable complete uptodate local/non-cloud desktop, independent of the exotic rare Android OTA rom update, which is already out-of-date before being transmitted. If you want a graphical presentation rather than my debian chroot do-it-yourself HOWTO sketch for rooted Androids, check out Canonical and Ubuntu for Android or have a physical look at Canonical's take on the Motorola Atrix.

This puts the GNU back into - hmmm - gnu(chroot)/Linux

... Newer Android Framework Updates by Custom-Rom to ...

To permit secure use outside the home (and within: defense in depth is certainly better than the classic one-packetfilter fits all hard-shell-soft-core LAN idea), a device also needs uptodate and secure methods to access the new applications, that is an uptodate GUI with at least current versions of VNC-Viewer (for access to current apps in the chroot), browser and ssh.

As Android Market doesn't permit marketplace links/updating for rom apps (180+ on a modern Android 2.3.6 device), this is a problem-in-waiting that can only be delayed by moving to custom roms, at the latest when the device ages out of vendor support. Still the same broken Android idea of big all-in-one-updates, but at least an available, less tardy, less broken and more secure (Android-creators of the 2012-safari-cross-domain-ads-crack-fame, device makers of Sony-Rootkit-fame, providers of carrier-IQ fame) option rather being forced to wait for the official channel OTA update, which many Android devices never had and never will see.

However, custom roms still suffer both from the compatibility limits of vendor-provided blobs (or possibly stock kernels, cf. Motorola Milestone) as well as the all-in-one update problem. Add Android fragmentation and custom rom support will wane later on, as will marketplace app availability. Even the chroot environment will suffer some of that bitrot, as newer and missing kernel features will preclude building or using some programs.

Note that custom rom availability further depends on vendor behaviour and device popularity.

(this corresponds to the current setup of my Note (debian chroot + custom rom), which - according to Samsung - will probably last until early 2013)

... Long-Term Supported Native Linux (possibly also Dual-Booting)

At this point in time, we can either happily accept any bugs and security flaws. Alternately we can vow to only use the device in totally secure protected environments in as secure a manner as possible. Like inside a locked bank tresor with the battery removed.

Or we can accept some loss of funtionality due to firmware blobs for uncommon and crippled hardware components (most likely candidates are sensors, maybe camera, and 3d acceleration, possibly also some wifi/gsm functionality) and consider switching (or dual-booting) to a native graphical interface and/or a native kernel (AOSP or official). Functionality loss should be less problematic on tablets, given typical usage. As for Call/phone/rild/gsm issues, they should become less problematic in the future, as providers ought to move to VOIP proper, as their transmission standards and protocols already do year by year.

By this we also extend proper package management to the equivalents of the rom / system apps and turn the chroot setup into the actual device setup: all programs - maybe excluding kernel, X11 server itself and a small number of libraries - can now be updated mostly independent of each other - no unnecessary delays introduced by needing to create / test / forge channel-wide legal arragements for the one and only single one-big-update-blob to bind them all (freezing features and bugs for at least a year until the next monster update; custom-roms at least drop lawyers and channel agreement constraints, but installing them is still a messy manual intervention with extra backup/restore steps, when compared to the use of a real package management).

By now our own official end of support only happens for either dying hardware/flash memory, change of user requirements, or device hardware being actively dropped from Xorg (/wayland(?)) and kernel source code.

Now we have solved the case and restored the proper environmentally sound and sustainable relationship of the descendants of archaeopteryx and artiodactyls to GNU/Linux.

However: there is one more step left for real bliss

Kernel and X server installation are probably still outside the package management or at least pinned to specific or custom-built versions.

For proper long term support, the results of working X11 / kernel modifications for a device also need to be pushed upstream into the official sources, and ideally also be packaged by embedded Linux distributions (which lag in tracking upstream), better yet by non-embedded classic GNU-Linux distributions like debian: a native setup of debian unstable installable with only a little manual intervention is the final perfect long-term device support scenario.

Otherwise, we're stuck at a series of badly-repeatable one shot demonstrations, that require some to huge effort ranging from rebuilding installs with manual flashing to long compiles with some patching to porting/rebasing old changes against current Xorg and kernel sources.

Currently all native efforts I've found - listed in Setup Alternatives - still seem stuck at the incomplete restricted functionality one-shot demonstration level.
